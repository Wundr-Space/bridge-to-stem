## Slide 2: Context / Problem

### The Generation Gap in STEM

**The Statistics Tell a Stark Story:**
- **9%** - Low socioeconomic background representation in UK tech
- **26%** - Women in STEM workforce (fewer than 1 in 4)
- **65%** - White men dominate STEM roles

### Who's the Customer?

**Primary:** Major STEM/Tech companies (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta, Deloitte, PwC, KPMG)
- Social Mobility Index Top 75 employers
- Public D&I commitments and ESG reporting requirements
- 1,000+ UK employees with diverse workforce

**Secondary:** Public secondary schools
- 30%+ free school meals (FSM) eligibility
- "Requires Improvement" OFSTED ratings
- 900-1,500 students, overstretched careers provision (0.5 FTE)

### Current Pain Points

**For Corporates:**
- ‚ùå Cannot demonstrate **authentic social mobility impact** beyond donations
- ‚ùå Employee volunteering is **ad-hoc, uncoordinated, unmeasured**
- ‚ùå Work placements **blocked by safeguarding/insurance barriers**
  - Example: Microsoft only allows placements for employees' children
- ‚ùå Generic schools outreach **doesn't activate diverse employees**
- ‚ùå No way to track: "Did our CSR investment change student outcomes?"

**For Schools:**
- ‚ùå **No capacity** to coordinate employer engagement (0.5 FTE for 1,500 students)
- ‚ùå Students get **low-quality work placements** (local shops, hairdressers)
- ‚ùå **No alumni network** or relatable role models
- ‚ùå **OFSTED criticism** of weak community engagement

**For Students:**
- ‚ùå Never met anyone in STEM from similar background
- ‚ùå Perception: **"Tech is for posh kids, not people like me"**
- ‚ùå Social isolation being "only one" (only girl, only Black student, only working-class)
- ‚ùå Career-closing decisions made **without information** at ages 14-16

### How They Currently Try to Solve It

**Corporates:**
- Generic STEM ambassadors (no relatability focus)
- Donations to Code First Girls, Stemettes (arm's-length, no employee engagement)
- Employee volunteering platforms like Benevity (disconnected from schools strategy)
- Graduate apprenticeships (age 18 - too late for perception change)

**Schools:**
- One-off career talks through personal contacts
- Parents' careers day (limited professional networks)
- Generic STEM outreach (no "someone like me" factor)

**Result:** None of these create the relatable connections that actually change student outcomes.

### Why This Problem is Urgent Now

**Critical Decision Point:** Students make career-shaping GCSE choices at ages 14-16
- Without exposure to relatable STEM role models, talented students close doors permanently
- Once GCSE subjects are chosen, redirecting to STEM pathways becomes extremely difficult

**ESG Pressure:** Companies face increasing stakeholder pressure for measurable D&I outcomes
- Social Mobility Index rankings drive competitive pressure
- Investors demand quantifiable social impact, not just donations

**Untapped Resource:** Companies have diverse employees who want to give back
- First-generation professionals remember struggling with lack of guidance
- ERG members measured on "impact hours" but lack structured pathways
- Companies can't activate this hidden asset

### Emotional & Economic Impact of Inaction

**For Students:**
- Talented students from working-class backgrounds miss STEM opportunities entirely
- Perception barriers ("not for people like me") perpetuate inequality
- Economic: Lifetime earnings gap between STEM and non-STEM careers

**For Corporates:**
- Reputational risk: D&I commitments seen as "just optics"
- Missed talent pipeline: homogeneous recruitment continues
- Economic: Wasted CSR budgets with no measurable ROI

**For Society:**
- Tech workforce remains 91% non-low SES, 65% white men
- Social mobility stagnates
- Innovation suffers from lack of diverse perspectives
